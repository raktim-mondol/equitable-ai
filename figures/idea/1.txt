Panels share a consistent colour scheme (e.g., blue = advantaged group, orange = disadvantaged group). Each panel includes a miniature plot or diagram and a one‑line caption explaining the metric. The figure can be done entirely with vector graphics (TikZ, matplotlib, or an illustration tool like BioRender). I’ll describe the panels in detail. --- ### Panel 1 – TPR / FPR disparity **Diagram:** Two side‑by‑side bar charts, each showing TPR (true positive rate) for Group A and Group B. - Group A bar is taller (e.g., 0.90), Group B bar is shorter (0.72). - A double‑headed arrow between the tops marks the “TPR gap”. - Add a small confusion matrix for each group (optional) to show how TPR is computed. **Caption:** “TPR disparity = max TPR – min TPR. Here a gap of 0.18 indicates unequal recall between groups.” --- ### Panel 2 – Subgroup AUROC gap **Diagram:** A single ROC plot with two ROC curves. - Curve for Group A (blue) is closer to the top‑left, AUC = 0.89. - Curve for Group B (orange) is lower, AUC = 0.73. - Shade the area between the curves or draw a horizontal bracket marking the AUROC gap (0.16). - Diagonal line in grey for reference. **Caption:** “Subgroup AUROC gap = |AUC_A – AUC_B|. A gap means the model discriminates better for one group.” --- ### Panel 3 – Equalised odds / Equality of opportunity **Diagram:** Two bar charts showing TPR (for equal opportunity) and TPR + FPR (for equalised odds). - Draw a horizontal “target” line that both groups should meet. - Show that after threshold adjustment, Group A and Group B now have identical TPR (equal opportunity) or identical TPR and FPR (equalised odds). - Use arrows to indicate the shift in decision threshold for each group. **Caption:** “Equalised odds: TPR and FPR equal across groups. Equality of opportunity: only TPR equal.” --- ### Panel 4 – Demographic parity **Diagram:** Simple bar chart of “positive prediction rate” (P(ŷ=1)) for two groups. - Without parity, bars are unequal (e.g., 60% vs. 30%). - A dashed “fair” line shows the average rate. - Add a caution symbol (⚠) and text: “Ignores true prevalence differences; use with care.” **Caption:** “Demographic parity: equal fraction of positive predictions across groups, regardless of ground truth.” --- ### Panel 5 – Expected calibration error (ECE) & subgroup calibration gap **Diagram:** Two calibration plots (observed event frequency vs. predicted probability) side‑by‑side. - For Group A, points lie close to the diagonal (well‑calibrated, ECE = 0.03). - For Group B, points systematically deviate (e.g., predicted 0.8 but observed only 0.6), ECE = 0.18. - Highlight the “subgroup calibration gap” as the difference in ECE. **Caption:** “ECE measures miscalibration. Subgroup calibration gap = |ECE_A – ECE_B|; rarely reported (0 studies).” --- ### Panel 6 – Worst‑group accuracy **Diagram:** A bar chart of model accuracy for four or five predefined subgroups (e.g., White male, White female, Black male, Black female). - Mark the shortest bar (worst‑group) with a red circle. - Show an overall accuracy line above, highlighting the drop. **Caption:** “Worst‑group accuracy = min(accuracy across subgroups). A direct proxy for service equity.” --- ### Panel 7 – Preserved‑site cross‑validation **Diagram:** A schematic of data splitting. - Show three “site” blocks (Site 1, Site 2, Site 3). - In standard CV, samples from all sites are mixed and splits are random (illustrate with interleaved coloured points). - In preserved‑site CV, each site’s data remains entirely in one fold (illustrate with blocks: fold 1 = Site 1, fold 2 = Site 2, etc.). - Add a small AUROC bar that drops from 0.80 (standard CV) to 0.51 (preserved‑site). **Caption:** “Preserved‑site CV: each site stays in one fold, preventing data leakage and exposing site‑confounded performance.” --- ### Panel 8 – In‑distribution vs. out‑of‑distribution (OOD) gap **Diagram:** Two overlaid histograms or a scatter plot, with “source” and “target” domains. - In‑distribution (ID) accuracy is high (e.g., 0.92). - OOD accuracy is lower (e.g., 0.70). - Annotate the drop: “22.9 pp gap on CAMELYON17” (as in your paper). - Show a few example images (e.g., a clean histology slide vs. one with different staining). **Caption:** “ID‑vs‑OOD evaluation separates seen from unseen sites/scanners. A large gap signals generalisation failure.” --- ### Panel 9 – Bias proxy metrics (site‑classification accuracy, mutual information) **Diagram:** - **Left side:** a small neural network diagram with input patch → feature extractor → site‑probe head. Show that the probe achieves 96% accuracy at identifying which site a slide comes from. - **Right side:** a mutual information (MI) bar comparing two features: MI(label, center) = 1.47, indicating strong site‑label dependency. **Caption:** “Bias proxies: site‑classification accuracy (high = features contain batch signal) and mutual information (quantifies site‑label confound).” --- ### Putting it all together You can arrange these nine panels in a 3×3 grid on a single page.
