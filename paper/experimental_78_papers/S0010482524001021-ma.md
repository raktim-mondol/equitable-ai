## ABSTRACT

In histopathology practice, scanners, tissue processing, staining, and image acquisition protocols vary from center to center, resulting in subtle variations in images. Vanilla convolutional neural networks are sensitive to such domain shifts. Data augmentation is a popular way to improve domain generalization. Currently, state-of-the-art domain generalization in computational pathology is achieved using a manually curated set of augmentation transforms. However, manual tuning of augmentation parameters is time-consuming and can lead to sub-optimal generalization performance. Meta-learning frameworks can provide efficient ways to find optimal training hyper-parameters, including data augmentation.

In this study, we hypothesize that an automated search of augmentation hyper-parameters can provide superior generalization performance and reduce experimental optimization time. We select four state-of-the-art automatic augmentation methods from general computer vision and investigate their capacity to improve domain generalization in histopathology. We analyze their performance on data from 25 centers across two different tasks: tumor metastasis detection in lymph nodes and breast cancer tissue type classification.

On tumor metastasis detection, most automatic augmentation methods achieve comparable performance to state-of-the-art manual augmentation. On breast cancer tissue type classification, the leading automatic augmentation method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art manual data augmentation.

## 1. Introduction

Computational neural networks (CNN) are capable of achieving human-level performance in a variety of complex computer vision tasks [1,2], including medical imaging [3], on data sufficiently similar to training data. However, vanilla CNNs performance is known to significantly deteriorate if a shift exists between distributions of training and test data [4,5], typically referred to as domain shift. In natural images, the source of domain shift can be related to a literal change in the image content (e.g. bird classification in temperate vs. tropical climates), but also due to changes in image acquisition (noise, change in illumination, change of camera). In medical imaging, domain shift can occur when testing on data from different centers, where each center has its own image acquisition devices and protocols. This is especially prominent in computational pathology due to multiple sources of domain shift [6].

The process of obtaining whole-slide images (WSI) consists of several steps. First, tissue resulting from biopsies or surgeries is fixed and paraffin-embedded. Subsequently, the tissue is sliced and stained with

various dyes. The dyes highlight the underlying tissue composition under the microscope. The most common stains currently used in histopathology are hematoxylin and eosin (H&E). Hematoxylin highlights the acidic components, such as nucleic acids in dark blue. Eosin, in turn, is responsible for the pink color of base components that constitute the surrounding tissue. Each of these preparation steps can differ between labs, from fixation time, tissue thickness, staining temperature, and pH, to name a few. After tissue preparation and staining, slides are digitized with a whole-slide scanner, which, depending on the brand, has different optical characteristics and post-processing filters.

All these differences affect the final image's appearance. Fig. 1 illustrates variation among whole-slide image patches originating from different institutions. The image samples were taken from the TIGER challenge dataset [7] for the identification of various tissue types in breast cancer biopsies and resections. Straightforwardly applying convolutional neural networks trained on data from a single center to such data is bound to fail badly [8]. Developing methods robust to changes in image acquisition is, thus, essential to achieve good generalization.

Various approaches have been proposed to improve domain generalization in computational pathology, e.g. stain normalization, domain adaptation, mapping the data into domain invariant space, data augmentation, etc...

Stain normalization encompasses techniques aimed at reducing color and intensity variation among slides from various labs. Reducing the variation is usually accomplished by matching the color properties of a set of images to a target image domain. Stain normalization techniques can be split into two categories: classical and machine learning-based. Among classical ones, [9] proposed to convert color value space to optical density space, in which the H&E stains are linearly separable. [10], proposed a method based on statistical analysis to impose the color characteristics of images from a source to a target domain. As for machine learning-based methods, [11] focuses on matching the characteristic distributions of source and target slides via classifying pixels into various stain components. Generative adversarial networks have been used to learn complex nonlinear mappings between domains: stain normalization can be seen as a style transfer problem, in which the stain characteristics from an external center are transformed to the domain of the target one [12–14]. A benefit of using stain normalization is the decoupling of the primary task, i.e. classification, and domain invariance objectives. However, an additional step is then required before classification. Stain normalization methods were shown to suffer from instability and leave behind site-specific features in images [15].

In domain adaptation techniques, it is assumed that representation learned from a source domain can be adjusted to a new target domain. [16,17] proposed techniques to align the distribution of source and target domain taking into account task-specific decision boundaries, utilizing adversarial and contrastive learning correspondingly. [18] assumes that CNN convolutional filters are domain-invariant while domain-specific layers capture the domain variability. Domain adaptation can thus be achieved by tuning the mean and standard deviation parameters of batch normalization. One disadvantage of the aforementioned methods is that they require unlabeled data from the target domain. A recent proposal for H&E slides to fine-tune and validate the model with data from every new source.

Graziani et al. [19], Lafarge et al. [20] use domain-adversarial training to learn domain-invariant representations for H&E slides. In domain-adversarial training, an additional classifier (domain discriminator) is involved in the training pipeline. During training, the weights are optimized to maximize the accuracy of the target domain-specific features that do not contribute to the main task, are removed from the representation. A drawback of such methods is that it requires considerable modifications of the initial training architecture, and it is

not possible to ensure robustness to variations not encountered during the domain-adversarial training.

Data augmentation is a commonly used solution to introduce a certain extent of invariance to a CNN [22–26], it has the capacity to introduce auxiliary biases to the model [27]. Data augmentation has been extensively used in computational pathology to improve inter and intra-domain generalization [28]. The commonly used augmentation techniques are attempting to encode prior knowledge about possible variations encountered in the data and could be subdivided into two classes: morphological and color augmentation. The morphological augmentations are, for example, rotation, shear, Gaussian blur and noise, and scaling. These can help robustness against scanning variations such as postprocessing with sharpening filters. Color augmentation such as perturbation of values in various color spaces (RGB, HSV, LAB) or modeling of stain densities [29], aims to simulate variations in the staining process and scanner color characteristics. [5] conducted a comprehensive study comparing various strategies using morphological and color augmentations to tackle domain shifts in histopathology. They focused on curating an optimal set of manually selected and parameterized augmentations for diverse tasks in computational pathology.

However, data augmentation is not without its downsides. For example, it typically requires selecting a task-dependent set of potential augmentations. Specifically, one should not use transforms that affect the separation between classes, e.g. a scaling transform might not be ideal if we want to discriminate between different tissue types in which nuclear size is a defining characteristic.

Furthermore, manually tuning the hyperparameters of the selected transforms is time-consuming, cumbersome, and also task-dependent. Even if we would reduce all hyperparameters in each transform to a magnitude (i.e. the intensity of the transform) and a probability (i.e. the chance of applying the transform to an image), this would require thousands of experiments to cover the search space of all possible combinations of augmentations and hyperparameters for a new task. To simplify the further discussion in the rest of the paper, a combination of a set of augmentations with associated hyperparameters (magnitude, probability) will be referred to as an ‘augmentation policy’.

## 1.1. Related work

In recent years, several meta-learning frameworks for automatic CNN hyperparameter optimization appeared [30–32]. Among them, there are also methods particularly aimed at optimizing data augmentation policies, as shown in Fig. 2.

Fig. 2. In conventional training of a classifier, data augmentation hyperparameters are manually curated by a human. The augmented data is passed to the classification CNN, which is then trained via gradient descent. In a meta-learning pipeline, the data augmentation hyperparameters are found by iteratively optimizing (meta-optimizer) an objective function (meta-objective) on a finite augmentation search space. The data augmentation hyperparameters that correspond to an optimum of a meta-objective function are then used to train the final model.

These methods either focus on significantly reducing the search space or utilize efficient search algorithms to select beneficial augmentation policies. [33] proposed AutoAugment, a framework for automated augmentation policy selection based on reinforcement learning. In AutoAugment, a controller recurrent neural network predicts an augmentation policy from the predefined search space; a child network is trained until convergence with this pre-selected policy achieving accuracy, R. This accuracy R is then used as a reward to update the controller gradients. Thus, as training progresses, the controller learns to generate more beneficial policies. AutoAugment achieved state-of-the-art performance on several public benchmarks. Nonetheless, its application to other tasks is challenging due to the extreme computing cost, as it essentially requires the training of numerous task-specific CNNs [34] to optimize the recurrent network.

Subsequently proposed automatic augmentation methods improve upon AutoAugment by considerably decreasing the computation cost. Population-based augmentation (PBA), proposed by [35] uses a combination of evolutionary algorithms and population-based training [30] to find an optimal augmentation schedule. In Online Hyperparameter Learning for Auto-Augmentation, [36] the data augmentation policy is optimized jointly with network parameters. [37] view data augmentation as filling missing data points of the original training data distribution. They define the search for optimal augmentation policy as the minimization of the distance between augmented and original data distributions via adversarial learning. Fast AutoAugment [38] utilizes density matching to find effective augmentation policies. In Differential Automatic Data Augmentation (DADA) proposed by [39], the data augmentation policy selection is modeled as sampling from a discrete distribution. The favorable augmentation policies are found via differentiable optimization with gradient approximation. Last, RandAugment [34] reduces search space to only two hyper-parameters: the number of sequentially applied transforms and transform magnitude, the optimal values of which are then found via grid search. In our previous work [40], we found that sampling transforms from a continuous uniform distribution improves the performance of RandAugment on H&E stained histopathology data.

## 1.2. Overview

In this work, we select four state-of-the-art automatic data augmentation methods from the literature and investigate their usability in improving generalization and robustness to domain shift in computational pathology on H&E stained slides across varying tasks.

The contributions of this work are the following:

1. 1. We compare the performance of four state-of-the-art methods from literature in improving generalization on 'domain-shifted' data in H&E stained histopathology. We make the optimal augmentation policies, schedules, and hyperparameters found with all investigated methods, as well as our source code publicly available athttps://github.com/DIAGNImegen/pathology-he-autoaugmentation.

### 3. Methods

#### 3.1. Data augmentation

Data augmentation generally refers to a process of complementing existing data with modified copies with an aim to increase diversity in a finite dataset. The modifications that are used often simulate possible natural variations occurring in data. In histopathology, such variation could be captured to a certain extent by applying transforms perturbing particular image properties i.e. contrast, brightness, color, sharpness, etc. In this study, we use 22 different transforms with varying magnitudes as shown in Fig. 3. Conventionally, augmentation hyperparameters i.e. magnitude, probability of each transform were manually curated by humans relying on intuition, which can lead to sub-optimal performance. In recent years, a number of meta-learning frameworks were proposed, which allow the selection of various training hyperparameters (including data augmentation) automatically.

#### 3.2. Automatic data augmentation

In the scope of this study, we explore four automatic augmentation methods. The selected methods propose diverse approaches to augmentation hyperparameter search, achieve state-of-the-art performance on well-known computer vision benchmarks, e.g. ImageNet [42] or CIFAR [43], their hyperparameter search source code is publicly available and they are widely used in the computer vision community. Similarly, these methods were developed for general computer vision and analysis of natural images. In this work, we explore their usability and performance on H&E stained histopathology images (see Table 1).

#### 3.2.1. Population based augmentation1

Population-based augmentation (PBA), proposed by [35], uses Population-based training [44], a method for automatic hyperparameter selection, which combines random search and a mixture of evolutionary algorithms to find an optimal data augmentation schedule for a particular task.

Initially, a population of models is trained in parallel for a preset number of epochs with randomly sampled data augmentation hyperparameters. Afterwards, the models are evaluated on a test set and the exploit & explore phase begins. During exploit, weights of top 25% models are copied to bottom 25%, in this way, weak members of a population “exploit” the strong ones. During explore, augmentation hyperparameters are either resampled or their original values are perturbed. Subsequently, training resumes until the next round the exploit & explore phase. In such an evolutionary manner, only the weights and hyperparameters of the best models are passed on to the next generation. During the training process, the augmentation hyperparameters which resulted in better performance at a certain epoch are saved, allowing the creation of a schedule. This schedule can then be used in subsequent training runs, omitting the search phase.

### 3.2.2. Fast augmentation

In Fast Augmentation,4 proposed by [38], the augmentation policy selection is performed via Bayesian optimization. The training dataset is split into model data, D_M and augmentation data, D_A. The model is trained until convergence on D_M and frozen. In the exploit & explore phase, for each step t \in [1, T], B candidate policies are explored, where B = T_1 \dots T_N, through Bayesian optimization. Particularly, a sequence of sub-policies is sampled repeatedly from the search space S and applied to D_A. The trained and frozen model is inferred on transformed D_M and evaluated to construct a policy T = T_1 \dots T_N. The corresponding probabilities p_{T_1} \dots p_{T_N} and magnitudes \lambda_1 \dots \lambda_N are tuned to minimize the loss L on an augmented dataset D_A. This set of operations is repeated, while the top-N performing transforms are appended to the final set of transforms. T. The final set of transforms, T, is used to train the model. The augmentation process is repeated first split into K = 5 folds and the set of policies is found for each fold and then merged together to form the final policy. In this study setup, we search for policies for each pseudo-fold separately without splitting the data in folds during the search, to assure fair comparison of methods, for details see Section 4.1.

### 3.2.3. RandAugment

Unlike the rest of the methods in this study RandAugment5 [34], instead of utilizing search algorithms to speed up hyperparameter search, it relies on significantly reducing the number of search hyperparameters. In RandAugment the search space is reduced to only two dimensions for all transforms: magnitude (m) and a number of consecutively applied transforms (n). Initially, a set of K augmentation transforms is defined, where each of the transforms has a uniform probability to be selected, 1/K. The final search space of the method consists of a linearly parameterized set of magnitudes \lambda and the number of sequentially applied transforms n. The method subsequently proposes to find optimal parameters m_{\text{opt}} and n_{\text{opt}} for a particular task, dataset, and network through grid search.

In this study, it was shown that using a range of magnitudes m = [0, m_{\text{max}}]. Instead of a single value m = m_{\text{opt}} can potentially improve generalization model in H&E stained histopathology. In this study, we use a uniformly sampled magnitude, m = U(0, m_{\text{max}}), in RandAugment experiments.

### 3.2.4. Differentiable automatic data augmentation

In Differentiable Automatic Data Augmentation6 (DADA) [39], similar to FastAugmentation a Softmax parent, a search space consists of policies that contain a set of sub-policies, each of which contains two types of transform operations, each of which has a magnitude and a probability of being applied. The authors parameterize the selection of which sub-policies are applied via a categorical distribution. Furthermore, the probability of each augmentation within a sub-policy being applied is modeled as a Bernoulli distribution. These distributions are made differentiable by augmenting a Softmax parentization process. For augmentations where the magnitude is non-differentiable (e.g. translation, rotation), the authors use a straight-through gradient estimator. As the entire data augmentation stack is now differentiable, the parameters can be optimized with gradient descent during network parameter optimization. Last, to prevent biased gradient estimates, the authors use an unbiased gradient estimator, RELAX [45].

## 4. Experiments

### 4.1. Data split

Successful optimization of deep learning frameworks relies on the assumption that training, validation, and test data belong to the same distribution [46]. This way, improving performance on validation data means achieving a better degree of generalization. When the goal is to achieve cross-institutional generalization, using validation data coming from the same institution as the training data is flawed i.e. the validation performance will not translate to the real-world, making this setup highly sensitive to overfitting. In this work, we assume that a model trained on data from institution a and validated on centers b, c is capable of generalizing to data from new unseen institutions. Thus, we arrange the experiments as follows: we always train the model exclusively on data from a single center (Randoul University Medical Center, RUMC), the validation set contains two external centers, while the test set consists of data from the remaining centers. The datasets in validation and testing are subsequently permuted composing into pseudo-folds, see Table 2.

### 4.2. Training details

For all of the auto-augmentation methods, we defined a common set of augmentation transforms with their minimum and maximum ranges, see Fig. 3. From the commonly used set of transforms [34] for computer vision applications, we excluded “invert” and “interpolate” transforms, as we earlier found they can degrade the performance of algorithms in histopathology by introducing unrealistic examples [40]. In addition, we used a set of transforms with histopathology-relevant augmentations, namely H&E “blur”, “HSV/shift”, “scaling”, “Gaussian noise”, “Gaussian blur”, “elastic transformation”. For all the experiments we used ResNet18 [47], a cross-entropy loss function. Adam optimizer [48] with a learning rate of 1e^{-4} and a weight decay of 1e^{-6} based on the values of validation loss with a patience of 10 epochs, batch size of 64 patches and patch size of 128 \times 128 px. All models were trained for 100 epochs, and the model with the lowest validation loss was selected as the final.

#### 4.3. Baseline

As a baseline for comparison, we chose the highest performing manually-tuned classification policy identified in [8], namely ‘heal-light’: a combination of classical and modality-specific data augmentation methods. Additionally, for reference, we provide a comparison to a model trained without any augmentation.

The ‘heal-light’ augmentation policy from [8] included: 0°, 90°, 180°, 270° rotation; vertical and horizontal flips, scaling factor ∈ [0.8, 1.2]; elastic deformation with parameters α ∈ [80, 120] and σ ∈ [9.0, 11.0];

HED channel perturbation with magnitude ratios α ∈ [−0.05, 0.05] for all HED channels; brightness with magnitude ratio ∈ [0.65, 1.35]; contrast with magnitude ratio ∈ [0.5, 1.5]; additive Gaussian noise with σ ∈ [0.0, 0.1]; Gaussian blurring with σ ∈ [0.0, 0.1]. All transforms were applied to each patch in the training set. The transformed magnitude was sampled from a uniform distribution (ranges mentioned above) for each patch in the training set.

We use the area under the receiver-operating characteristic curve (AUROC) as the main evaluation metric, consistent with [8, 11, 49].

### 5.3. Computational cost

AutoML methods are known to carry a large computational cost, for instance, performing data augmentation hyperparameter search with a 2000-fold cross-validation on reduced CIFAR-10 dataset would require 3500 GPU hours NVIDIA Tesla 33xx – a computational cost that prohibits broader use of the method. One of the standard ways of comparing the computational cost of different methods is to compare the floating-point operations (FLOPs). While FLOPs give a precise estimate of the computational complexity, due to differences in implementation this is impractical to compare. Another strategy could be to compare

cost in GPU hours, but for each of these models was implemented on different hardware, making these comparisons also infeasible. Instead, we chose to compare on the relative search space and compute cost compared to the baseline, i.e. how many baseline models need to be trained to obtain optimal data augmentation policies. This is shown in Table 5. The search space size delineates a number of options that need to be explored by a meta-optimization process during the hyperparameter search phase. The computational cost allows us to approximate the relative order of magnitude of the computational cost of methods per task.

Among all of the algorithms studied here, RandAugment explores the smallest hyperparameter search space (< 10^7), however, it relies on grid-search to find optimal hyperparameters, making it the least computationally efficient out of all automatic augmentation search methods. Fast Augmentation, PBA, and Fast Augmentation explore different types and sizes of search space, they carry a comparable computational load. DADA is the most computationally efficient out of all the algorithms in this study, which it explores identical search space to Fast Augmentation.

### 5.4. Human effort

Computational resources are not the only parameter that signifies how much time it takes to obtain optimal augmentation hyperparameters with a particular augmentation method for a specific problem. In addition to computational resources, extra effort from the side of a researcher is required to adjust different methods to a particular task and application. We provide an indirect estimate of the necessary researcher effort required to enable the usage of a particular automatic augmentation method for a new task.

Table 6 provides a comparison of automatic augmentation methods with a particular task. For example, to use third-party dependencies the necessity to perform the search as a proxy task, additional search hyperparameters that might need to be adjusted to use the method on a new task. We report third-party dependencies used by various methods, they often impact their usability in further applications. In PBA, Fast Augmentation and DADA search is performed in a separate from the training phase – proxy task, which considerably complicates the training. In addition, the proxy task has extra hyperparameters, the values of which might differ from that to task, e.g. a number of sequentially applied transforms in Fast Augmentation and DADA or a number of children models in PBA. On the contrary, RandAugment does not require a search on a proxy task, which simplifies its use.

Table 7 indicates which parts of an automatic augmentation framework need to be modified to perform a search for optimal augmentation parameters. In order to perform a search, only modifications to data processing part of an existing training pipeline, PBA, Fast Augmentation, and DADA require adjustment of multiple parts of the automatic augmentation scheme. As Fast Augmentation is an inference time search method, it does not require adjustment of training hyperparameters of an automatic augmentation.

## 6. Discussion

In this study, we compare four state-of-the-art automatic data augmentation methods and investigate their applicability in improving generalization and robustness to domain shift in H&E stained computational pathology. We compare the performance in two tasks, across 25 different histopathology laboratories. The automatic augmentation methods can achieve results comparable to those scrupulously curated by a human. The augmentation policies learned by automated methods on a particular task (CAMELYON17) transfer to other tasks, but the results are not as good as those manually curated, see Table 4. As shown by [51], optimal augmentation policy is specific to a particular dataset, and model architecture. While automatic methods can be easily readjusted to a new task, the same

Comparing the different methods, we found that they all outperform models trained without data augmentation and are relatively competitive with each other in terms of performance. As such, other considerations such as computational cost and ease of use also warrant consideration when selecting a method for a computational pathology project. We found PBA to have disadvantages in both compute cost and performance, with a large standard deviation on results. While Fast Autoaugmentation is considerably slower than DADA, the convergence criteria in DADA are vaguely defined. In [39], during the search phase, the network is optimized along with augmentation hyperparameters, and optimal augmentation is considered to be found after 20 epochs; however, there is no particular quantitative trend in metric or loss values, that would signify the occurrence of an optimum. Thus, further analysis needs to be done to reduce the ambiguity of obtaining optimal augmentation policy, especially if one wants to transfer it to other tasks.

RandAugment achieves state-of-the-art performance and requires minimal human efforts to be adjusted to a new task, however, it bears a relatively high computational cost. Additionally, in RandAugment, each transform has an equal probability of being applied, while in reality, some augmentations can potentially be more useful in one domain and task than others. Possible ways to decrease the compute cost would be to define a search grid more sparsely or utilize a more efficient search algorithm such as Bayesian search.

Despite our expectations, in this study setup, we did not observe a trend or pattern of a ‘‘perfect augmentation’’ to which all of the studied methods converge. That being said, consistent with [8], we observed domain-specific augmentations being particularly useful in improving generalization in H&E histopathology, e.g., color augmentation transforms appeared in every automatic augmentation method policy.

Finding optimal data augmentation is a complex and entangled problem, even only within the scope of H&E stained histopathology. RandAugment has two hyperparameters: different magnitudes for all transforms (m) and a number of sequentially applied transforms to each image (n). In [51] it was shown that the smaller the dataset is the more augmentation is needed (higher m; higher n); the larger the network, the more augmentation is needed (lower m; lower n). In this study, the network and its hyperparameters were fixed. For the CAMELYON17 dataset, the optimum occurred at n = 5 and m = 21. RandAugment RQ dataset has a larger number of hyperparameters and differences in optimal hyperparameters can be due to a wide variety of reasons: different tissue and tasks (cancer in breast tissue versus metastasis in lymph nodes); different resolutions (0.50 \mum/pixel versus

0.25 \mum/pixel); different initial staining of training slides, i.e. even if slides originate from the same center of the batch effect can still be present; the diversity in training data (different number of patients presented); training data for each task; levels of noise in datasets, i.e. in TIGER data the classes ‘‘invasive tumor’’ and ‘‘in-situ tumor’’ were merged into a ‘‘cancerous epithelium’’ class and ‘‘tumor-associated stroma’’, ‘‘thyroid glands’’, ‘‘necrosis not in situ’’, ‘‘inflamed stroma’’ and ‘‘rest’’ were merged into the ‘‘other’’ class. Overall, the optimal RandAugment hyperparameters for CAMELYON17 and TIGER are relatively close to the optimal hyperparameters for the other settings that the optimal augmentation policies are similar for similar datasets [33]. Thus, when seeking optimal RandAugment hyperparameters for a new task in H&E stained histopathology, it might be beneficial to use hyperparameters found on CAMELYON17 or TIGER as a starting point.

While all of the studied automatic augmentation methods can generate augmentation strategies that improve domain generalization in H&E stained histopathology, they also carry a number of shortcomings. On one hand, RandAugment, having only two hyperparameters, offers a very logical design, but it has a limited scope to explore with various transforms being assigned the same value of magnitude. On the other hand, the rest of the methods search for sub-policies where each transform is assigned a suitable value of magnitude, thus, at the same time leads to a significant expansion of search space to 10^7 options and above. For future work, it would be useful to explore the continuum between these two extremes.

While the availability of publicly released source code improves reproducibility, it does not guarantee it. The reliance on third-party libraries and software without specifications of versions complicates adjusting automatic augmentation methods such as Fast Autoaugmentation, PBA, and DADA to new applications.

In the scope of this study, we fix network architecture and hyperparameters across all investigated methods to allow for a fair comparison. In future work, it would be valuable to explore how the architectural choices, initialization, and other hyperparameters impact domain generalization. In addition to augmentation transforms used in this study, such transforms as Cutout [52], Mixup [53] and CutMix [54], etc. are widely used in general computer vision, and it would be useful to investigate how these methods impact domain generalization in computational pathology.

Additionally, there is a growing need for large diverse multi-institutional datasets to train machine learning applications in computational pathology to enable wider validation of domain generalization of machine learning methods.

While domain adaptation, stain normalization, domain-supervised training, and augmentation methods offer effective solutions to improve domain generalization, none of these methods can guarantee domain invariance per se. Thus, in future work, it would be valuable to investigate the prospects of stain-equivariant convolutional neural networks, similar to rotation- and translation-equivariant networks [49, 55].
