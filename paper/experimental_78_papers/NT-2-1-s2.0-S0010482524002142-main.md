**ABSTRACT**

Artificial intelligence (AI)-assisted diagnosis is an ongoing revolution in pathology. However, a frequent drawback of AI models is their progression to make decisions based rather on bias in training dataset than on concrete biological features, thus weakening pathologists' trust in these tools. Technically, it is well known that microscopic images are altered by tissue processing and staining procedures, being one of the main sources of bias in machine learning for digital pathology. So as to deal with it, many teams have written about color normalization and augmentation methods. However, only a few of them have monitored their effects on bias reduction and model generalizability. In our study, two methods for stain augmentation (AugmentHist) and fast normalization (HISnorm) have been created and their effect on bias reduction has been monitored. Actually, they have also been compared to previously described strategies. To that end, a multicenter dataset created for breast cancer histological grading has been used. Thanks to it, classification models have been trained in a single center before assessing its performance in other centers. This setting led to extensively monitor bias reduction while providing accurate insight of both augmentation and normalization methods. AugmentHist provided an 81% increase in color dispersion compared to prominent augmentations only. In addition, every classification model that involved AugmentHist presented a significant increase in the area under receiving operator characteristic curve (AUC) over the widely used RGB shift. More precisely, AugmentHist-based models showed at least 0.14 AUC increase over RGB shift-based models. Regarding normalization, HISnorm appeared to be up to 7% faster than conventional methods. It also provided satisfying results in terms of bias reduction. Altogether, our pipeline composed of AugmentHist and HISnorm improved AUC on biased data by up to 21.7% compared to usual augmentations. Conventional normalization methods coupled with AugmentHist yielded similar results while being much slower. In conclusion, we have validated an open-source tool that can be used in any deep learning-based digital pathology project on 1088 whole slide images (WSI) that efficiently reduces stain-induced bias and later on might help increase pathologists' confidence when using AI-based products.

## 1. Introduction

Microscopic analysis relies on the observation of tissue slices using a high-resolution microscope to detect and characterize structural anomalies are stained using hematoxylin and eosin (H&E). Actually, safranin and eosin have been used to stain the nuclei of cells with a blue color while eosin highlights cytoplasm and connective tissues in pink. Despite the use of the same chemical compounds, the actual color slices strongly vary from laboratory to laboratory, and the intensity and particular staining protocols from different laboratories cause H&E images to be non-comparable. In addition, a slide color can be inferred from its stain characteristics. Besides, color intensity is altered by the age, and sample preparation and less contrasted than more recent ones. Altogether, these variations represent a frequent source of bias in digital pathology.

Machine learning (ML) algorithms rely on the assumption that the training dataset accurately represents the diversity of real-world data. Unsurprisingly, it has been observed, especially in histology, that data in which bias is often induced by overrepresentation of frequent or limited diversity of data. Both the training and the testing of datasets. Such drawbacks can trigger overfitting, giving rise to ML models that are unable to generalize to new data. It is worth mentioning that in clinical research, patients' cohorts are statistically correlated to prognostic features on the basis of both inclusion and exclusion criteria. In short, the selection of patients can produce a bias when building ML models.

It is paramount to ensure that the data augmentation, which consists in modifying images using either geometric (rotation, translation, crop), color (contrast, saturation, brightness, hue, gamma) or morphological level transformations acting on images has are commonly used to make model predictions less reliant on such [1]. Unfortunately, these transformations often generate unrealistic output, creating noisy data that cannot exist in real world. Although little noise is welcome in ML, the presence of noise in medical data can hinder their performance. Some attempts have been proposed to reduce standard deviation in data reconstruction [2] on realistic real-world color variability [3] and create realistic images. Beside data augmentation, another approach to reduce the bias is to use color normalization. While data augmentation artificially expands a dataset during training, normalization rather reduces variability at inference time. More specifically, normalization modifies color values in images to make it easier to make a decision at that of a reference dataset or image. This method can be used at training and with data augmentation [4] or at inference. Even though it seems counterintuitive to concurrently use augmentation and normalization, the two methods can be concurrently used around a reference point before being spread using augmentation. This enables normalized images to be integrated into the training color space [5]. However, several approaches to color normalization have been already reported. Most relied on the estimation of a color deconvolution and then on a link between H&E stains and RGB space [6]. More recent methods used Mt. to identify morphological structures and associate them with one specific stain [7–9]. In addition, some authors used variational autoencoders [10] or generative adversarial networks [11] to perform color normalization. These methods reached a reference stain [12–18]. Although these methods have been widely used in digital pathology, only a few studies described their actual performance using histology [19,20].

In our study, we have developed innovative methods for stain augmentation (StainAug) and fast and accurate color normalization. We revisited Vahadane's method [6] to separate hematoxylin and eosin stains and to be simpler to implement. We also improved the color methods on his reduction has been studied and compared to other widespread techniques, therefore we used a multi-stain dataset created for histology. We further highlight the importance of classification models were trained with single color images before assessing its performance on images from other color images. Finally, we used to extensively monitor bias reduction, thus providing accurate assessment of our augmentation and color normalization methods.

**2.4. H&E: deep learning-based method for H&E images normalization**

**2.4.1. Nomorad dataset**

The Nomorad dataset is a 700 fully-anonymized WSI collection of breast tissue slides gathered in the Pathology Department of the University Cancer Institute of Toulouse - Oncopole. This WSI acquisition was done in a short period of time in order to avoid any substantial change in specimen handling, slicing technique and staining protocol. Therefore, this collection was composed of homogeneously stained WSI breast tissue. These WSI were then divided into contiguous 1024 × 1024-pixel patches filtered to remove background, creating

more than 64,000 images. This dataset was then split into training and validation sets. Tissue samples were collected and processed at the “CRB Cancer des Hôpitaux de Toulouse”, following ethical procedures (Declaration of Helsinki). In accordance with French law, the “CRB Cancer des Hôpitaux de Toulouse” Cancer collection was reported to the Ministry of Higher Education and Research (DC 2009-889). A transfer agreement (AC-2008-820) was obtained following approval by the ethics committees.

**2.4.2. Model**

H&E is a deep learning (DL)-based normalization method that is trained using Nomorad dataset and AugmentH&E. At training time, images were randomly re-stained using AugmentH&E before being transferred into H&E space using a sci-kit-image [26] implementation that we adapted for PythonR [27]. The H channel has been used alone as input to a U-Net model [28] expected to reconstruct the original non-augmented H&E image, since the most relevant features of tissue architecture lie in this channel. As a matter of fact, hematoxylin counterstaining is widely used in routine practice since it allows an accurate appreciation of tissue architecture mainly in tumoral tissues in which tumor cellularly allows pathologists to capture these patterns. Nevertheless, there might be some exceptions such as collagen-rich tissues or some parasitic infections in which key image patterns lie in the eosin channel [29]. Our method forced a full H channel remaining that often exhibits most of the stain variability. The whole model is illustrated in Fig. 2.

2.5. Effectiveness measurement of AugmentHE and Hémon on bias reduction

In order to measure the effectiveness of AugmentHE and Hémon and Hémon on bias reduction we focused on a single task: histological grade classification of breast cancer. We chose to focus on monitor 1 and grade III distinction since we have essentially wanted to monitor bias reduction on a straightforward and intuitively simple classification task. The classification of grade I versus grade III BC has already been clinically relevant [33]. Particularly, our goal was to discriminate between histological grades I and III on the basis of H&E WSI. Our aim was also to test whether AugmentHE and Hémon improved the generalization ability of a model trained on biased data, instead of reaching high accuracy since automated grade assessment remains challenging.

2.5.1. Data

To that end, our dataset was split up depending on the source of the images cohort. As it contained most of these images (Supplementary Table 1), PCAS04 was used for training, whereas PCAS05 and PCAS08 were kept for biased test. Ten percent of PCAS04 images were put aside for validation while the remaining 90% were divided into 5 folds for k-fold cross-validation. Tissue detection was then computed using thresholding in the L*a*b* space on WSI thumbnails, before extracting contiguous 1024 × 1024-pixel patches in foreground regions. This resulted in about 420,000 PCAS04 patches, being 80,000 histological grade I and 340,000 histological grade III respectively. In order to face this data imbalance, we randomly undersampled grade III images during training step, showing up a difference of less than 2% in class populations. This is to say that only 40,000 images were used at each epoch. During the inference step, 20,000 PCAS05 patches (3000 grade

1 and 17,000 grade III) and 28,300 PCAS08 patches (800 grade I and 27,500 grade III) were applied.

2.5.2. Model

We tried different combinations of bias reducing techniques, all including at least geometric augmentations, in order to compare them between each other. Our baseline used only geometric augmentations such as flips and transpositions (noted as basic). We compared it to a classical augmentation method called RGB shift which applies a random offset to the values of each RGB channel (noted as rgb_shift). We then evaluated both AugmentHE and Hémon separately (respectively noted as augHE and Hémon) and both combined (noted as Hémon_augHE). In order to compare Hémon to the state of the art, we evaluated combined AugmentHE and Vahadane’s normalization method [6] (noted as Vahadane_augHE). For each of these combinations, we trained 10 ResNet50 [34] classifiers, using two series of 5-fold cross-validation. Backpropagation was performed using binary cross-entropy loss and AdamW optimizer [31]. We used a One Cycle [32] policy with a maximum learning rate of 2 × 10
^-4^

### 5.4. Combination of both AugmentH and Hiznorm contributed to bias reduction

When it comes to ML algorithm training, cohorts containing images from different origins should be indistinguishable in order to avoid any bias. To that end, it is common to chain normalization and augmentation, also leading to wide enough data dispersion so as to prevent overfitting. Intuitively, applying these transformations in reverse order would be counterproductive as normalization should cancel out stain augmentation. This is confirmed in Fig. 3, where point clouds obtained when using AugmentH before Hiznorm looked alike those obtained using Hiznorm only. Furthermore, ASD only increased by 21% when adding AugmentH before Hiznorm. In contrast, when using AugmentH only, ASD was 137% higher than with Hiznorm. Conversely, adding Hiznorm before AugmentH only decreased ASD by 12%, with the added benefit of a near perfect overlap between point clouds and its centroids. Consequently, the use of Hiznorm followed by AugmentH looked like the optimal combination for training step. Practically, the main goal of our study was to monitor generalizability of models trained on biased datasets applying different combinations of data augmentation and normalization. ## 6. Discussion

Two innovative approaches for data augmentation (AugmentME and color normalization (Hifnorm) have been developed in our study, providing an end-to-end pipeline for histological image preparation within DL projects. Respective impacts of AugmentME and Hifnorm have been assessed and compared to widely used techniques previously described in the literature [4–6]. The first efficient combination of both AugmentME and Hifnorm regarding bias reduction has been proposed. As pathological images are often affected by AugmentME, pragmatic color augmentation method based on our daily practice in laboratory context made it possible to achieve the tangible benefits of the training process as a broad source of color variability. It was also greatly inspired by Tellez et al. [13] as their HE2 augmentation helped us to improve our knowledge about the impact of color process on the actual augmentation pipeline. Thanks to it, we generated quite convincing and visually biologically relevant images. In this process, using color augmentation methods based on non-specific approaches such as RGB or hue shifts, More importantly, a significantly data bias reduction was obtained with AugmentME compared to classical methods. Furthermore, Hifnorm appeared to be a fast and reliable method for color normalization that considered the major role of coin composition in color variation of H&E slides. Actually, rosin is frequently used with certain types of celloidsine that drastically modify the hue of H&E images from one laboratory to another. In addition, rosin fades with time and light exposure rather than hematoxylin, leading to color variability. In our case, the color variability is shown to be more stable and harbor most of the characteristics about color variability that can be considered as relevant for H&E image reconstruction. Accordingly, Hifnorm is a DL-based H&E generator from a single input image that can be used to expand our input data. In terms of results, Hifnorm efficiently decreased color dispersion from one cohort to another and was much faster than every other method based on DL [4–6]. Interestingly, according to generalizability, our method significantly reduced bias when combined with AugmentME compared to PACS08 images. This result was achieved considering that regarding PACS08 cohort in which Valadzadeh’s method combined with AugmentME achieved the best results, the impact of color normalization from color normalization, neither Hifnorm nor Valadzadeh’s method alone could fully reach bias reduction expectations. Therefore, it clearly appears that color normalization could not be regarded as a fine tuning tool applied at the end of ML model development to further reinforce the performance of the model. Besides, results obtained on combined PACS05 and PACS08 than on each cohort alone. It is a clear illustration that good results can hide bias if they are not properly analyzed through multiple lenses. In this study, we have shown how to apply AugmentME and Hifnorm by exploring every possible combination in order to achieve the most optimal performance of our bias mitigation. We therefore showed that the best way to bring the hue of cohorts closer to the target is to apply augmentation methods on each augmentation than it considerably reduced stain-induced bias. Besides these satisfying results, several options should still be considered as potential enhancements of the pipeline. Firstly, AugmentME could be further improved to more realistic modelization of color variations between laboratories. In fact, we chose to enlarge color dispersion on the basis of our laboratory staining color spaces. Another option could have been to directly represent the variability between laboratories. This difference is still no precise knowledge available. In practice, defining such variability would have been a challenge. Indeed, the variability is not acceptable representation of real-world variability. Secondly, in terms of color normalization, Tellez et al. showed that the mix of several normalization methods can help to reduce bias [13]. The latter study corroborates our results showing that there is no universal normalization method that can be applied to all cases. Thirdly, potential improvements on Hifnorm method that would rely on advanced learning methods such as GANs, Diffusion models or Transformer MTL loss, which has been perfectly designed for mixed representation despite not being state-of-the-art. Therefore, this reconstruction may benefit from adversarial loss, as generated images should not be distinguishable from the original ones [13,35]. Actually, such adversarial techniques have already been described in the literature [13,25,18], and demonstrated better results than classical methods did [36]. We admit this is one out our study’s main limitations and the impact of those more recent methods on our results remains to be investigated, not with AugmentME, should be addressed in future works. Another limitation is that our study did not consider the impact of data classification performance only at patch level, without aggregating results at image level. Indeed, the H&E heterogeneity might be more important for variations in grade at patch level that should be accounted for when aggregating data at WS level [37]. However, it is important to emphasize that our study’s main purpose was to monitor bias reduction through some normalization and augmentation methods, including our own. Consequently, performance achieved at patch level was sufficient to assess the impact of various combinations of data augmentation and normalization. Furthermore, recent papers have moved away from the concept of grade to directly classify tumors based on the presence or absence of early BC recurrence [38]. However, we speculate that these approaches might be more challenging than our bias reduction tools, such as those proposed herein. Concerning the fact that our study was conducted on breast cancer images, we believe that it can be applied to other cancer types. On the one hand, AugmentME is already applicable to every kind of H&E histological images. On the other hand, Hifnorm is not a universal method, but its principle is being modified, or might need an additional training based on targeted images. In our case, we propose to use Hifnorm in the same way as it is in rare cases in which key image patterns lie in the soin channel, such as melanoma diagnosis [39]. In this case, Hifnorm could be used in research should be carried out in order to validate Hifnorm in these cases.

## 7. Conclusion

Our work is thus part of a general breakthrough in ML history where there is a growing interest in the study of bias after a long period of neglect. Accordingly, in the field of histopathology, we have shown that, indeed, we quantified stain-induced bias in a multisite dataset as well as the effectiveness of several augmentation and normalization methods on our own. According to the fact that nowadays datasets are hardly ever optimally designed for ML studies, we propose a pipeline that can be used to mitigate bias in a multisite dataset. This pipeline should also serve as a baseline for future proof of concepts that need a ready-to-use bias-reduction method as well as new studies on bias in digital pathology. We think that the versatility of our pipeline is not only designed for color bias reduction in breast cancer studies but also the setup of our pipeline can be applied to other cancer types.
