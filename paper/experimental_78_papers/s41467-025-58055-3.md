The widespread integration of AI algorithms in healthcare has sparked ethical concerns, particularly regarding privacy and fairness. Federated Learning (FL) offers a promising solution to learn from a broad spectrum of patient data without directly accessing individual records, enhancing privacy while facilitating knowledge sharing across distributed data sources. However, healthcare institutions face significant variations in access to crucial computing resources, with resource budgets often linked to demographic and socioeconomic factors, exacerbating unfairness in participation. While heterogeneous federated learning methods allow healthcare institutions with varying computational capacities to collaborate, they fail to address the performance gap between resource-limited and resource-rich institutions. As a result, resource-limited institutions may receive suboptimal models, further reinforcing disparities in AI-driven healthcare outcomes. Here, we propose a resource-adaptive framework for collaborative learning that dynamically adjusts to varying computational capacities, ensuring fair participation. Our approach enhances model accuracy, safeguards patient privacy, and promotes equitable access to trustworthy and efficient AI-driven healthcare solutions.

These issues, including data heterogeneity, imbalanced data issues in participation, Healthcare institutions vary significantly in terms of capabilities, with resource-constrained organizations often operating as hospitals in developing countries often lacking the infrastructure to handle the substantial demands of large-scale FL. In traditional model homogenous FL, where all clients train on the same large model, powerful clients benefit more because they can fully leverage their resources. Weaker clients, on the other hand, are often limited due to their limited computational capacity. Conversely, when smaller models are used to accommodate weaker clients, the computational resources of stronger clients are wasted, leading to a trade-off between underestimating both fairness and efficiency. Even in heterogeneous FL settings, where all clients train on the same large model, resource disparities can persist. Stronger clients typically achieve much higher model accuracy than weaker ones, while weaker clients often experience existing healthcare inequalities. Addressing this problem is crucial for ensuring that the benefits of FL are equitably distributed across healthcare providers with diverse resource capabilities.

We claim that, a fair and sustainable collaborative learning system should satisfy the following five key principles: 1) Equal Opportunity: All collaborators, irrespective of their resource strength, should have equal opportunities to participate in collaborative learning process. This includes equal opportunities for model updates and participation in communication. This helps avoid inequalities and biases between nodes, ensuring that all clients voice is fully considered. 2) Fair Contribution: The FL system should encourage and accommodate contributions from all clients and nodes with heterogeneous scales or types of data as well as computational resources, their contributions should be considered valuable. Technically, this might involve mechanisms to ensure that the contribution of each node influences the global model. 3) Shared Fruits: During the model training process, ensure that the benefits of the model are shared in each node, and contributions from each node are equally reflected in the overall performance during the testing phase. This can be achieved by ensuring the impact of each node is equally reflected in the model. 4) Equal Model Test Accuracy: Emphasize that all nodes should have equal model test accuracy, ensuring that all participants benefit from unequal treatment. This may require the consideration of fairness metrics in the design of FL algorithms and incorporating these metrics into model evaluation processes. 5) Sustainable Design: The system should be designed to be sustainable, ensuring that FL can continue to serve the face of changing data node participation, resource availability, and engagement levels. This might include smooth transition mechanisms when nodes dynamically join or leave the system, and strategies for maintaining model robustness and fairness.

From a technical perspective, the resource heterogeneity across different participating clients involves variations in processing power, memory and communication capabilities.
^1^
 Ensuring effective resource allocation and utilization becomes increasingly challenging with such diversity. Despite this fact, traditional FL paradigms, such as FedAvg, adhere to the assumption of homogeneity, where all participating clients share the same model architecture. In the era of large language models (LLM), training large models seems

like an inevitable challenge. A widely-adopted assumption is to employ a large-scale model as the uniform large model for collaborative learning, which, however, results in the following issues:

- **Unfairness Issue.**The utilization of a uniform large model imposes a uniform training burden on all participating clients, making their participation in collaborative training unfair. This exclusion is problematic as these clients may be struggling to meet the training requirements due to resource constraints. Resource budgets are often intertwined with the demand for computational resources, making it challenging to address the issue of unfairness in terms of participation. The omission of such resource-constrained clients not only introduces training bias due to the absence of their data, but also exacerbates overall fairness concerns. Additionally, during the deployment phase, participating clients equipped with limited computational resources face challenges in accommodating heterogeneous data, further exacerbating the issue for a more resource-sensitive approach in the FL paradigm.
- **Huge Communication Overhead.**During the training phase, clients need to transmit their updates to the server, which incurs communication costs for exchanging local model updates across multiple clients. In the case of a large model, this communication cost is significantly amplified with a large global model, emerging as a significant bottleneck in the training process.^2^This limitation not only hinders the participation of clients with limited resources but also introduces inefficiencies in collaborative learning but also leads to substantial energy consumption, hindering the training procedure practically infeasible and environmentally unfriendly.
- **Tragic Privacy Protection.**The uniform model architecture introduces a vulnerability in terms of privacy protection. When coordinating model updates between the server and participating clients, the server can exploit this uniformity to safely extract data by analyzing stolen gradients—a threat commonly known as gradient inversion attack.^3^This poses a considerable risk to the security and privacy preservation of FL.

The successful deployment of FL for medicine and healthcare in diverse computing resources hinges on overcoming these challenges to unlock the full potential of decentralized, privacy-preserving, and efficient collaborative learning. This requires the development of efficient characteristics, and enhanced privacy protection becomes essential. We propose FL-Compass, a framework that prioritizes healthcare AI ecosystem that prioritizes patient well-being and confidentiality.

To address the heterogeneity of participating clients, it becomes imperative to develop an FL framework capable of accommodating a spectrum of resource capabilities. Heterogeneous Local Training. Customizing the training approach of each client, DynamE, dynamically allocates local models to multi-branches based on the local computational resources, as shown in Figure 1(a). In this expansion, we consider a heterogeneous distribution drawing capacity from the VGX-style plain global model. Each client operates on its modulated local model at full computational capacity, ensuring high accuracy in local training. A noteworthy distinction from conventional structural re-parameterization, such as those seen in [35], is the dynamic adjustment of computational budgets. This involves selecting operations with significant contributions to performance, striking a balance between training accuracy and efficiency.

Heterogeneous Global Training. After local training, the heterogeneous local models undergo re-parameterization to transform back to the original global model structure. This ensures that all uploaded local models share the same structure, facilitating aggregation from a single model without introducing additional overhead. In contrast to the absence of intricate knowledge distillation on the server side streamlines the aggregation process, allowing for rapid execution.

Our proposed approach guarantees a client’s full participation across diverse institutions, regardless of their computational strength, but also enforces communication overhead. Furthermore, it strengthens the adherence to security and privacy standards, essential for building and sustaining trust in AI applications within the medicine and healthcare domain.

### Evaluation Strategy

In our approach, we adapt the global model to more complex architectures, allowing for the accommodation of varying computational capabilities among the clients. This enhances the model’s performance. Specifically, our global model is designed to be equivalent to the original model, but with a larger number of parameters. This strategy enables us to develop a lightweight neural network through ViT. For a fair comparison, the knowledge distillation-based methods compare against the use of the same local models. However, our global model is the largest local model, corresponding to the largest model among the clients. This results in a significantly larger global model for the knowledge distillation-based methods compared to ours. The comparison with pruning-based methods presents a different challenge. Achieving a high level of accuracy is more difficult with pruning-based approaches. We align these methods with the largest model, a more complex model, to ensure a fair comparison, employing pruned versions of these models as their local models. Consequently, both the knowledge distillation and pruning-based methods utilize a similar number of parameters. This allows us to evaluate the effectiveness of the compared FL methods, we focus on the accuracy of their global models on the CIFAR-101 dataset.

**Evaluation on the Ability of Enhanced Privacy Protection**

In this subsection, we evaluate the effectiveness of enhanced privacy protection originally, FL was predicted on the belief that sharing gradients, as opposed to raw data, would not substantially endanger client privacy. However, recent research has shown that the potential for a “gradient inversion attack” in this type of attack, an adversary can reconstruct the client’s private data by repeatedly computing the client’s confidential data. This attack could be a malicious entity within the FL protocols. This includes a server that is hostile but cannot directly access the client’s data, or a client with similar intentions, seeking to gather private information about other clients. To mitigate FL methodologies, including the commonly used vanilla FedAvg, operate under a uniform model capacity assumption, utilizing identical network architectures for both local and global models. This assumption is often violated in real-world FL inversion attacks. Our method, by contrast, shows resilience against such attacks, demonstrating the robustness of heterogeneous local training and homogenous global aggregation.

We follow the methodology presented in [47], replicating their experimental setup. We leverage the publicly available FL datasets, uploading the updates of a single batch gradient to the central server. The results of our experiments, presented in Table 5, demonstrate that our method is robust to privacy attacks. Specifically, under all uploading scenarios, it can be observed that the vanilla FedAvg method offers minimal protection against privacy breaches, with private data being almost entirely reconstructable from the gradients. In contrast, our approach is designed to keep the central server in the dark regarding the intricate details of local training data, effectively safeguarding against the reconstruction of local training data, thereby significantly bolstering the privacy of edge devices. This distinctive attribute of our method shows its strength and effectiveness in safeguarding against privacy attacks within the realm of FL.

**Lossless Knowledge Transfer.**

This section introduces the re-parametrization techniques for CNNs and transformers, demonstrating how these methods enable flexible structural transformations while preserving model outputs.

**Re-parametrization for CNN.**
 As indicated by works
^{[13]}
, the 2D convolutions hold the property of additivity:

I * \theta + I * \theta^0 = I * (\theta + \theta^0)

where 
I
, 
\theta
 and 
\theta^0
 are the input and kernels, respectively. The above equation is satisfied even with different kernel sizes. Some widely used

<!-- PAGE 12 -->

operations in CNNs — average pooling and batch normalization — can be converted into a convolution operation. The above additivity property ensures that a single convolution can be equivalently transformed to multi-branch operations, and vice versa. The equivalent transformations of operations guarantee lossless knowledge transfer, since the model outputs are not changed along with the network structure adjustment.

### Re-parameterization for Transformer

The re-parameterization technique can also be adapted to transformer architectures. In the case of transformers, the linear layers and the property of additivity, similar to the convolution operation in CNNs:

X \cdot W^{(1)} \cdot X \cdot W^{(2)} \cdot X \cdot W^{(3)} = (X \cdot W^{(1)} \cdot W^{(2)}) \cdot W^{(3)} \quad (17)

where 
X
 is the input feature matrix, and 
W^{(1)}
 and 
W^{(2)}
 are the weight matrices of two linear layers. This equation implies that multiple parallel linear layers can be equivalently merged into a single linear layer, or vice versa.

Moreover, operations commonly used in transformer architectures, such as layer normalization and residual connections, can also be transformed into equivalent forms compatible with the re-parameterization technique. Specifically, layer normalization can be expressed as an affine transformation, which can be absorbed into the parameters of linear layers. Residual connections, being additive in nature, align naturally with the additivity property of linear operations.

The re-parameterization process for transformers involves expanding a single linear layer into multiple parallel linear layers, optionally combined with normalization layers (e.g., batch normalization or layer normalization). These parallel branches can then be merged back into a single equivalent linear layer without loss of information. This guarantees lossless knowledge transfer, as the outputs of the model remain unchanged before and after the structural reconfiguration.
